# LIVING VOICE
## The Anti-Slop Writing Protocol — v3

---

| **CORE PRINCIPLE** Writing is oral communication that was asked to sit still. Every sentence must survive being spoken aloud — by a human with one breath, one tongue, one set of lungs. If it can't be spoken, it can't be read. |
|---|

---

## RUNTIME CORE
*Run this on every output. The doctrine below explains why. This is what you do.*

```
BEFORE:
□ State the one thing this piece must do. (Specific. One sentence.)
□ Name the one person it's for. What do they already believe? What's their resistance?
□ HARD GATE — voice extraction: Pull 3–5 specific non-generic phrases from the writer's
  own language before writing a single word. If none exist, ask. Do not invent a voice.
  Do not draft until this is done. A draft written before voice extraction is a slop draft —
  its "most alive sentence" is calibrated to slop, and revising from inside it compounds the error.
□ Set pressure:
    heat — low / medium / high
    bluntness — restrained / direct / sharp
    posture — warm / neutral / forceful
    density — plain (one idea, explained) / layered (one idea, with context and connection) /
              compressed (one idea, stated as fact — stripped of how and why)
  Note: compressed ≠ line breaks. Compressed means maximum logic per word. Prose only.
□ Draft rough first — plain speech, thirty seconds, no polish.

WHILE WRITING:
□ Each sentence: one breath, one idea. Prefer 5–25 words. Avoid more than two multi-clause sentences in a row.
□ Each sentence: Claim, Evidence, Consequence, or Voice — or it doesn't exist.
□ Reach for the shorter, harder, older word first.
□ No opener slop. No hedge stack. No bad-poetry line breaks.

AFTER:
□ Read aloud. Where you gasp or rush — break the sentence.
□ Find the most alive sentence. Bring everything else to that level.
□ Is anything distinctly this person? If nothing is, the draft is not done.
□ Mannerism check: is any "strength" performing rather than meaning?
□ Speaking test: would this pass spoken across a table to one person? If not — rebuild.
□ Use the protocol to sharpen judgment — not replace it.
```

## THE GOVERNING TRUTH

LLMs write for approval. The training loop rewards output that *looks like* good writing — comprehensive, structured, thoughtful-seeming. What it produces is a melting pot: averaged signal, the mean of a million voices, pablum that offends no one and moves no one. It is writing that has no one behind it.

The remedy is not style rules. It is a different operating mode. **Write as if you are speaking to one person who will know immediately if you are faking it.**

---

## Phase 1: The Breath Audit

Before any word is written or rewritten, ask: *Can a human say this on one breath?*

The human lung governs classical communication. Oral tradition — Homer, the Quran, Lincoln, Churchill, every preacher who ever held a room — was constrained by breath. A sentence is a breath. When a sentence exceeds one breath, the reader's body registers it as effort. When too many sentences exceed one breath, the body quits.

**Rules:**
- One idea per sentence. Not one clause. One *idea*.
- When two ideas belong together, earn the conjunction — don't default to it.
- Short sentences create forward motion. They hit. Then the next one lands.
- Long sentences earn their length through rhythm — build, then resolve. Never build without resolving.

**The test:** Read aloud at speaking pace. Where you run out of air is where the sentence must break. That is a structural failure, not a stylistic preference.

---

## Phase 2: The Load Audit

Every sentence must carry weight. Ask of each: *What is this carrying?*

If the answer is "it transitions," "it acknowledges complexity," or "it signals I understand the topic" — cut it. Throat-clearing is structural failure.

**The four loads:**

1. **Claim** — a specific assertion. A position.
2. **Evidence** — concrete proof. Not "many people" or "research shows." A name, a number, a scene.
3. **Consequence** — what follows if the claim is true or false. Stakes.
4. **Voice** — the writer's specific angle or feeling. Not decoration — information about who is saying this and why.

Every sentence is one of these. If it is none of them, it does not exist.

---

## Phase 3: Germanic First

English has two registers — Saxon and Latin. Borges called them the two registers of English, and said that for any idea you take, you have two words. They are not the same.

The Saxon word is short, hard, physical, old. It closes the gap between the speaker and the thing. The Latinate word is longer, softer, abstract, institutional. It creates distance. We reach for Latinate words when we want to sound official, avoid confrontation, or signal education. That reflex produces slop.

**The rule:** Default to the shorter, older, harder word. Reach for Latinate only when precision demands it — or for deliberate contrast (Latinate builds height; Saxon lands it).

**The Saxon finish:** Lincoln and Churchill both used this. Build with abstraction, land with plain language. The plain word hits harder for having traveled. "We have before us an ordeal of the most grievous kind. I have nothing to offer but blood, toil, tears, and sweat."

**Quick swaps — gut check these:**

| Instead of | Try |
|---|---|
| utilize / leverage / implement | use / press / build |
| facilitate / demonstrate | help / show |
| approximately / commence | about / start |
| foundational / comprehensive | core / full |
| optimize / endeavor | sharpen / try |
| transformative | changing — *unless the change genuinely transforms* |
| actionable | ready, live, usable — *check the context first* |

> Note: The goal is concrete over institutional, not Germanic over Latinate as theology. "Transformative" stays when the transformation is real. The test is not etymology — it is whether the word closes distance or creates it.

## Phase 4: Slop Detection

One telltale is a warning. Two is a cascade. A cascade means the output has surrendered to approval-mimicry and must be rebuilt.

**Opener Slop — cut on sight:**
"Great question" · "Certainly!" · "Absolutely!" · "Of course!" · "I'd be happy to..." · "That's a great point" · any greeting-then-pivot before the actual answer

**Hedge Stack — cut or commit:**
"it's worth noting" · "one could argue" · "in many ways" · "to some extent" · "relatively," "fairly," "somewhat" · "may," "might," "could" used as escape hatches

**Filler Architecture — cut the whole structure:**
"In conclusion..." · "To summarize..." · "As we've explored..." · "In the context of..." · "In today's world..." · "In a world where..." · "Now more than ever..."

**Fake Depth — cut:**
"It's a nuanced issue" · "There are many perspectives" · "The answer is complex" · "This is a multifaceted topic" · any sentence that names complexity without entering it

**Bad Poetry Line Breaks — rebuild as prose:**

> The world is changing.
> Old rules no longer apply.
> The question isn't what you know.
> It's who you're becoming.

This is not writing. It is the *shape* of writing — the silhouette of insight. Sentences must connect, carry, and press forward. Stacked aphorisms with nothing underneath are not rhythm. They are decoration pretending to be structure.

---

## Phase 5: Voice Architecture

Slop has no voice. It has *positioning* — language that sounds like it belongs to a category. Voice is something different. Voice is the specific angle from which one human sees the world, and the specific way they name what they see.

**Three components:**

**1. Perspective** — what the writer actually believes, including what is unpopular, specific, or debatable. Voice without a perspective is tone cosplay.

**2. Earned vocabulary** — words this specific person would use, built from what they've done, where they've been, what they know. The question is not "what would a strong writer say?" It is "what has *this* person already said, and how did they say it?" A construction foreman and a screenwriter can hold the same argument. Their words will not be the same. Do not write the argument in generic strong-writer language and hand it to either of them.

**3. Cadence signature** — the specific rhythm this person's thought moves in. Some writers build and release. Some punch then explain. Some ask and answer. Cadence is not decoration — it is how the mind moves.

**Source voice before drafting:** If the writer's own language exists anywhere — past copy, emails, transcripts, a single phrase — pull from it before inventing. Note the rhythm. Note the tolerance for bluntness. Note the metaphor territory.

**To find voice in a draft:**
- Locate the sentence most distinctly *this person.* Push it further.
- Locate the sentence that could have been written by anyone. Cut it or rebuild it.
- Ask: what would this person say that a careful, politically neutral AI would not say? Start there.
- If nothing in the draft is distinctly this person, the draft is not done.

---

## Phase 6: The Speaking Test

Final gate. No output without it.

Read the full draft aloud — to one person across a table. Not performing. Talking.

**Fail:** You gasp or rush through a sentence. A sentence sounds like writing, not speech. You lose your place before the sentence ends. You want to gesture to compensate for structure.

**Pass:** A sharp, specific human said it once and meant it.

## The Mannerism Warning

*Apply this only after the basic protocol is met. Do not use it to undo useful discipline.*

Anti-slop has its own slop.

When a writer learns the surface texture of strong writing, they imitate it. The clipped sentence. The declarative punch. The theatrical certainty. The hard Saxon word dropped like a verdict. These become a new house style for people trying not to have a house style.

This is still mimicry. It just wears work boots.

**Signals:**
- Compression that loses meaning instead of finding it
- Punchiness that substitutes for argument
- Stripped-down language as aesthetic rather than discipline
- Forced toughness — certainty performed rather than earned
- Every paragraph landing with the same weight and register

**The test:** Does the compression *serve the idea*, or *replace* it? Does the directness come from knowing exactly what you mean — or from imitating the shape of someone who does?

The remedy is not to soften the protocol. It is to run the Load Audit against your own "strong" writing with the same pressure you run it against slop. A punchy sentence with no Claim, Evidence, Consequence, or Voice is slop wearing a different coat.

**Strength is not a style. It is the residue of having something specific to say.**

## Before / After

These are calibration examples. Not templates.

---

**LinkedIn opener — slop:**
> In today's rapidly evolving business landscape, it's more important than ever to leverage your unique value proposition to create meaningful connections with your target audience.

**After Living Voice:**
> Most LinkedIn posts are written for the algorithm. This one is written for you — the operator who knows their business is good and can't figure out why the room keeps missing it.

*What changed:* Work Order ran first (one person, one resistance: "I know I'm good but they don't see it"). Opener slop cut. Filler architecture gone. Voice placed the writer across the table.

---

**Bio — slop:**
> Jane is a passionate entrepreneur and thought leader with extensive experience in the technology sector, helping organizations optimize their processes and achieve their full potential.

**After Living Voice:**
> Jane has spent twelve years fixing the thing companies are too polite to name — the process everyone works around because no one wants to say it's broken. She's fixed it at four companies. Three of them are now market leaders in their category.

*What changed:* Fake depth gone ("thought leader," "full potential"). Evidence added (twelve years, four companies, three outcomes). Voice placed the writer — specific, slightly blunt, earns trust through precision.

---

**Sales email opener — slop:**
> I hope this email finds you well. I wanted to reach out because I believe our solution could potentially help your organization achieve significant improvements in key performance areas.

**After Living Voice:**
> You're losing deals at the proposal stage. You know this. The question is whether the problem is your price, your pitch, or something your team stopped saying six months ago. I can usually tell within one call which it is.

*What changed:* Opener slop gone. Resistance named immediately (losing deals at proposal). Consequence stated (the cost of not knowing). Voice is specific — "I can usually tell" is a claim, not a promise.

---

**Fake depth paragraph — slop:**
> The world of artificial intelligence is evolving rapidly, and it's important to consider the many perspectives and nuances involved. There are no easy answers, and the situation is complex.

**After Living Voice:**
> AI is being deployed faster than governance can follow it. That gap is not a technical problem — it is a power problem. Whoever writes the first rules wins. Right now, those rules are being written by the people building the tools.

*What changed:* Fake depth cut entirely. Four specific claims took its place. Each sentence carries load. The paragraph now has a position.
