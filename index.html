The Roaring 20s World Music · 1920–1929

The 1920s were a Cambrian explosion for music — perhaps the most compressed burst of musical creation in all of human history, happening simultaneously on every inhabited continent.

Several forces converged at exactly the same moment to make this possible. The phonograph and radio were new enough to be revolutionary but mature enough to reach mass audiences: by 1929 there were 10 million radios in American homes alone, and a single performance could reach millions simultaneously for the first time ever. The music of a Harlem speakeasy, a Buenos Aires milonguero, or a Cairo takht ensemble could now travel across oceans on a shellac disc.

The Great War (1914–1918) had shattered the old world and left survivors hungry for release. Prohibition drove American nightlife underground and inadvertently turned jazz into the music of liberation — outlawed alcohol and outlawed joy found each other in the same basement. In Paris, Berlin, and London, artists who had survived the trenches threw themselves into the most radical music imaginable. The Weimar Republic produced Kurt Weill and the Comedian Harmonists; Les Six in Paris dismantled Romanticism with cheerful irreverence; Stravinsky essentially invented neoclassicism in a single ballet season.

Mass migration was mixing cultures in ways that had never happened before. The Great Migration brought Black American music northward to Chicago and New York. Argentine tango absorbed the Italian immigrant's bandoneón. Shanghai's cabarets employed Filipino jazz bands and White Russian orchestras. The Ottoman collapse scattered musicians across the Mediterranean. Everywhere, displaced people carried their music into new contexts — and new music grew from the collision.

Jazz was the universal solvent. Wherever it landed — Paris, Tokyo, Cairo, Havana, Johannesburg — it reacted with local musical tradition to produce something entirely new: shidaiqu in Shanghai, ryūkōka in Tokyo, rebetiko in Athens, marabi in Johannesburg. The decade ended with the Great Depression slamming the window shut. What happened inside those ten years — 30 countries, 30 flowering music scenes, all at once — was never repeated.
