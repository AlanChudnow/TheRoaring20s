Friday night I put the piano against the wall and move everything else to the edges. That is how the room becomes a dance floor. By nine o'clock there is nowhere left to stand that isn't already occupied by someone moving.

My name is Nomvula. I run this shebeen in Doornfontein, which means I brew the beer, I hire the musician, I pay the police sergeant his monthly consideration so he finds reasons to be elsewhere on Friday and Saturday nights, and I keep order when order needs keeping, which is not as often as you might think. Men who have worked underground all week come here to remember they are still alive. They are not looking for trouble. They are looking for this — the music, the movement, the smell of something fermenting in the back room, other bodies close enough to feel.

The law says I cannot sell liquor. The law says many things about what I cannot do, what I cannot own, where I cannot go after curfew, which streets I may walk and which I may not. I have read the law carefully enough to know where its edges are. My shebeen is on the other side of every one of those edges. That is not an accident.

The musician tonight is a young man from Marabastad who plays the pedal organ with his whole body, rocking forward on the downbeat, pulling back when the phrase lifts. He plays marabi — the same three chords cycling, cycling, a loop that doesn't resolve because resolution is not the point. The point is the groove inside the loop, the way a good phrase can turn on itself four hundred times and feel different each time if the musician is good enough. He is good enough. He has been playing since eight o'clock and it is now past midnight and he shows no interest in stopping.

This music came from the mines and the mission churches and from American records someone played at a white family's house where a man I know worked as a servant. Jazz, ragtime, the church harmonies — all of it went into the same pot and what came out is what you hear in this room tonight. It is ours now. It came from everywhere and arrived here and became something that exists nowhere else.

I watch the room the way I always watch it — not at the dancing specifically but at the whole, the way a current runs through water. When the energy is right, the room moves as one thing. You feel it through the floor before you see it in the bodies. The men from the Witwatersrand mines who have been underground since Monday — they come in hard, carrying the week in their shoulders, and inside thirty minutes the music has taken something off them. That is what I am selling, if I am being honest. Not the beer. The permission to put it down for one night.

Around two in the morning there is a knock at the door that is not a customer's knock. I know the difference. I move the bottles under the floorboards, which takes forty-five seconds if you have practiced it, which I have. By the time the constable's head appears in the doorway the room looks like a prayer meeting. People seated, music stopped, everyone with the patient expression of people waiting for something that has nothing to do with liquor.

He looks around. He knows. I know he knows. He takes his envelope from my hand, nods once, and goes back out into the street. The door closes. The bottles come back up from the floor. The musician puts his hands back on the organ and finds the phrase exactly where he left it, without a pause, without searching.

The room starts moving again.

This is how it works. This is how it has always worked, in every place where people are told they do not deserve a room of their own. They build one anyway. They put music in it. They dance until morning and go back to work and come back the next Friday and do it again.

My grandmother brewed beer in the valley where she was born. Her grandmother brewed it before her. The law that says I cannot do this is forty years old. The knowledge of how to do it is older than anyone can count. I know which one is going to outlast the other.
