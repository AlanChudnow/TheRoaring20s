I come downstairs at ten, not before. Before ten the floor belongs to the men and their practicing, their arguments, their posturing at each other across the room. After ten it belongs to everyone, which means it belongs to me.

My father came from Genoa with nothing. My mother from Naples with less. They share two rooms upstairs with my uncle's family — eight people, one window, the smell of the docks coming through it whether the window is open or not. This building has forty families in it. On a Friday night the courtyard becomes a milonga — a dance gathering, the music's natural home — and the bandoneon comes out and for three hours the building forgets what it is the other six days of the week.

I know what people think about girls who come to these dances. I know what the priests say. I come anyway.

Here is what they don't tell you about these dances: the women decide everything.

A man cannot walk across the floor and ask you. That is not how it works here. He sits on his side of the room, you sit on yours, and if he wants to dance he catches your eye from his chair and gives the smallest nod — the cabeceo. Just that. A question asked in a breath. And you answer by holding his gaze, or you answer by looking away, and if you look away he never stood up, nothing happened, no one saw, no shame passes between you. He stays in his chair. You were never asked.

I have looked away from men who own buildings. I have held the gaze of men who own nothing. My mother would have opinions about this. I have not consulted her.

When you accept and come together it is close — closer than anything the Church permits and considerably more honest. Chest to chest. His right hand at my back reading every shift in my weight. The lead is small, almost invisible from outside, just pressure and release, a suggestion that I can follow or redirect. The best dancers give you room inside the embrace to answer back. The worst ones push. You know within four steps which kind he is.

The bandoneon — a German concertina that crossed the Atlantic and became the voice of Buenos Aires — does something to the air in this courtyard. That sound — reedy, mournful, somehow also full of appetite — it gets into your chest and pulls at something. The Germans who made this instrument never imagined what would happen when it landed in the hands of men who had lost everything and were trying to build something from what was left. This music is what grief sounds like when it decides to dance instead.

Across the floor I can see Tomás watching me. He is a good dancer, maybe the best here, and he knows I know it. He is waiting for the right moment to try the cabeceo. I let him wait a little longer. Not to be cruel — to be precise. The moment matters. The yes or no matters. In this room that is not nothing.

Outside this room I have no vote, no property, no say in what my father decides for my future. In here I have the cabeceo, and a man sitting across a courtyard who cannot move until I let him.

I hold Tomás's eyes.

He stands.
