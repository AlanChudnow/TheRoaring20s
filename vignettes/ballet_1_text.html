Backstage at the Théâtre des Champs-Élysées, June 1924. An hour before the premiere of Le Train Bleu. Serge Diaghilev finds Coco Chanel watching a dancer attempt an acrobatic lift in a wool jersey bathing costume and rubber-soled shoes. The dancer's feet are adhering to the stage boards.

"She's stuck," Chanel says.

"Momentarily."

"Serge. She's stuck to the floor."

He watches. The dancer wrenches free and completes the lift. He applauds once, quietly. "You see. Problem solved."

Chanel says nothing. She makes a note on the small card she carries.

Diaghilev moves to stand beside her. He is large, immaculate, the white streak in his hair catching the light. "The costume is perfect."

"The sole is wrong. I'll have something sent tomorrow."

"Tonight is the premiere."

"I know when the premiere is."

He watches another dancer cross behind them — tennis skirt, pearl earrings swinging. He looks pleased. She looks at the hem.

"A centimetre too long," she says. "She's compensating for it without knowing it."

"To me she looks wonderful."

"She looks wonderful. The hem is still wrong."

He smiles. This is the thing he finds genuinely useful about her and also faintly maddening. He gestures toward the stage, where Milhaud's score is being run through again — light, quick, slightly insolent. "What do you think of the music?"

"It moves."

"That's all?"

"That's enough. Ballet music that calls attention to itself is a problem for the dancer. This doesn't."

He considers this. Coming from her it is high praise, though it doesn't sound like it. "Cocteau wanted something grander."

"Cocteau wants everything grander. The subject is people on a beach. Grand would be wrong."

"The subject," Diaghilev says, with a slight emphasis, "is the emptiness beneath the leisure. The beautiful surface concealing nothing."

She looks at him. "The people in those seats tonight will be in Deauville next month doing exactly what's on that stage. Have you thought about that?"

"Of course."

"And?"

"They can afford to laugh at themselves. That's what money is for."

She makes another note. "The earrings are too large. They'll read as costume from the third row."

"They're supposed to read as costume. It's a ballet."

"They're supposed to read as the earrings a certain kind of woman wears to the beach. There's a difference."

He is quiet for a moment. A dancer sweeps past in the bathing costume, free of the floor now, moving well. He watches with the focused attention he gives to everything that is almost right.

"You know," he says, "when I first asked you to do this, several people told me it was a mistake. That fashion had nothing to do with ballet."

"What did you tell them?"

"That they were confusing fashion with decoration." He pauses. "You're not decorating. You're making an argument."

She puts her card away. "I'm making clothes," she says. "The argument is yours."

The call comes for places. Diaghilev moves toward the wings with the unhurried certainty of a man who has done this a thousand times and still finds it extraordinary. Chanel stays where she is. She watches the last dancer disappear into the light, checking the line of the skirt until there is nothing left to check.

Then she follows the note about the sole to find someone who can fix it before tomorrow night.
