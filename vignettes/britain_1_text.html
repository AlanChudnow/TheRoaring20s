I have always considered myself a more than adequate performer on the dance floor. Not in the showy, look-at-me sense — I leave that to the Aunt Dalilas of the world — but in the solid, dependable, won't-disgrace-the-family tradition that has served the Woosters well across several generations. So when Bingo Little suggested we try Ciro's on a Thursday, I went along in a spirit of quiet confidence.

The confidence, I should say, did not survive contact with the Charleston.

Ciro's was doing its level best that evening. The place has a way of making you feel that modernity is not only possible but mandatory — low lights catching the gilt on the mirrors, tables packed close enough that you're essentially dining in each other's laps, the whole room wrapped in that particular cigarette-and-gardenias atmosphere that someone has clearly worked hard to manufacture. The girls were turned out magnificently: beaded frocks that caught the light when they moved, fringe doing its thing at the hem, headbands and ropes of pearls and shoes with heels that seemed structurally ambitious given what the dance floor was about to demand of them. The men were white-tied to a man, hair brilliantined to a high gloss. London, in short, had made the effort.

The band was eight pieces — trumpet, trombone, clarinet, piano, drums, banjo, and two others I couldn't identify from my angle — and they were playing American jazz in the way that London bands played American jazz in those days, which is to say competently and at approximately ninety percent of the required tempo, as if someone had told them the music was slightly dangerous and they'd decided to handle it with tongs. The effect was not unpleasant. It was the sound of a city trying on a new suit and not being entirely sure about the fit.

The thing about the Charleston is that it looks perfectly straightforward until the moment you attempt it, at which point it reveals itself to be something designed by a committee of Americans specifically to humiliate the British upper classes. The feet are supposed to go forward, then back. The knees come in, then go out. The arms do something that I can only describe as happening. I gave it my fullest attention and was half a beat behind almost immediately, in the manner of a man trying to board a train that has already decided to leave without him.

My partner — a Miss Somebody-or-other who was clearly made of sterner stuff — took matters in hand with the quiet efficiency of a person defusing something dangerous. She guided my timing, contained what she later described to Bingo as "the wilder impulses," and steered me away from the sort of collision that would have made the evening memorable for all the wrong reasons. For a few glorious bars I had it — I genuinely had it, the feet landing where feet are supposed to land, the whole business clicking into place — and then I overcorrected on a turn and she caught me with an expression I recognized as the one Jeeves uses when I've suggested something that requires him to say "I would not recommend it, sir."

I retired to the table feeling, on the whole, that I'd acquitted myself reasonably. This impression was not corrected by anyone present, which is, I've come to understand, the London way.

Then the door opened and everything changed.

They came in the way Americans come into rooms — not loudly, mark you, but with a sort of certainty, as if the room had been expecting them and they were prepared to forgive it for starting without them. Two of them: one blonde, one brunette, both in frocks that made the London girls' efforts look like a dress rehearsal — silk, I thought, cut closer than anything Mayfair had sanctioned, beading that caught even Ciro's careful lighting and threw it back with interest. The blonde one — Lorelei Lee, as I later learned — had a string of diamonds at her throat that seemed less like jewellery than a statement of intent. The brunette, Dorothy Shaw, had the look of someone who found the diamonds amusing and had said so once, clearly, and then let it go. Both with the look of women who had decided some time ago exactly what they thought of the world and found it, on the whole, entertaining.

They danced. I watched. The band, which had been playing at a kind of apologetic English tempo, seemed to notice something had happened and lifted itself accordingly — the rhythm sharpened, the sound brightened, and for a stretch of perhaps three minutes Ciro's stopped being a London club trying to be modern and became something else entirely, something with no self-consciousness in it at all.

Where I had interpreted the Charleston — made my best effort at it, negotiated with it, reached an uneasy truce — they simply inhabited it. The feet snapped. The timing was absolute. They weren't performing for the room so much as performing through it, the way a good wind goes through a house rather than stopping at the door.

After the set I found myself, through a series of social maneuvers I couldn't entirely account for, at a table with both of them. Lorelei had the quality of listening to everything you said as if it were more interesting than it was, which I found enormously pleasant. Dorothy had the quality of knowing exactly what you meant before you'd finished saying it, which was more unsettling but equally compelling in its way.

"You were dancing earlier," Lorelei said. It was not quite a question.

"The Charleston," I confirmed. "I had a go."

"We saw," said Dorothy, with a warmth that somehow made this worse.

We talked for another hour. They were, in the language of my set, tremendous fun — the sort of company that makes a room seem smaller in the best sense, as if everyone in it is suddenly much closer together. When they left, the band played on but something had gone out of the room. Not the music. Something harder to name.

I found Jeeves waiting with my coat at a quarter past twelve.

"Good evening, sir," he said. "I trust the dancing was enjoyable."

"Tremendously," I said. "Tremendous evening altogether. Met two American girls. Charming. Dorothy and Lorelei."

"Indeed, sir." He held the coat. "Miss Lee in particular has a considerable reputation as a dancer."

"You know her?"

"By reputation only, sir." A pause of the particular Jeeves variety that contains an entire paragraph. "I understand she is generally considered to be in a different category from most."

I thought about this in the cab on the way home, Jeeves having melted away by whatever method Jeeves employs when the evening's duties are discharged. Different category. Yes. That was, I decided, exactly right. I had been in the room when something happened, and I had met the people it happened around, and I had emerged intact and in reasonable spirits.

For a Wooster, I reflected, that was not nothing.
