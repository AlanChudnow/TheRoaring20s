James calls it the most modern city in Asia and I don't correct him. He has been in Shanghai four months. I have been here my entire life, which means I have watched the city he thinks he discovered get built by people who look like me, for purposes he has never been required to think about.

He holds the door and I step into the Paramount.

The music reaches you before your eyes adjust — shidaiqu, what the newspapers are starting to call the songs of the era. Li Jinhui's orchestra is somewhere at the front, the Chinese pentatonic melody riding over a jazz rhythm the way silk sits on water, touching everywhere and belonging to neither. The brass is American, the phrasing is not. You can hear both things at once if you know how to listen. James hears the brass. I hear the phrasing.

The room is already full. Electric light through amber shades turns everything slightly gold — skin, silk, cigarette smoke, the mirrors that line the walls and make the crowd seem larger than it is. Ceiling fans push the warm air in slow circles. Nothing in this room is accidental. Someone designed every detail to make you feel that time moves differently here, which it does, which is the point.

We find a table near the floor. Around us the usual geography: British traders and their wives near the entrance — the section with the better light and the easier path to the door, which they chose without noticing they were choosing. Chinese industrialists and their companions at the centre tables — qipao in silk and brocade, the new cut that shows the collar, the wrist, the ankle. My own qipao is green with small white flowers. My mother considered it too modern. I considered it the minimum required for this room.

Then Sofia appears.

Countess Sofia Belinskaya. I know who she is the way you know things in Shanghai — through the particular network of women who are paying attention. She arrived from St. Petersburg in 1918 with her daughter Katya and a coat with something sewn into the lining. Whatever it was, it is gone now. She carries a Nansen passport, which is what the League of Nations issues to people the Soviet government has decided do not exist. She is legally a citizen of nowhere.

Her husband's family — Princess Vera, Prince Peter, the whole magnificent ruin of them — live in three rooms in the French Concession and speak of their estates in the past tense at dinner parties Sofia pays for. They consider her work beneath the family name. They eat the food it buys without apparent contradiction.

She moves through the room with the unhurried precision of someone who has learned to read every table in thirty seconds. Her Russian is buried under an English so carefully neutralised it has no origin. Her gown tonight is midnight blue, French cut, three seasons old but worn as if it is not. She has the posture of a woman who grew up being watched and has decided, somewhere along the way, that being watched is simply the condition of her life and she will use it accordingly.

She materialises at James's shoulder. She pours. She laughs at something he says — not the laugh of amusement, the laugh of professional warmth, which is a different instrument entirely and which James cannot tell apart. She rests her hand briefly near his on the table. He is flattered. She is already calculating the next table.

Before she turns away our eyes meet. Not the social surface she showed James — something else, a half-second of complete clarity between two women who understand this room more precisely than the men they are sitting with. She has nothing I have and I have nothing she had. The revolution that took everything from her built the concession system that diminishes me. We are not the same. But we are both reading the room in a language the men at our tables do not speak.

Then it closes. She moves on.

On the floor, couples are dancing to the shidaiqu. A Chinese woman in deep red moves with a man I recognise from the Nanyang Brothers Tobacco Company, both of them inside the music the way you can only be when you grew up hearing it. James watches them with the expression of a man watching something beautiful he cannot quite reach. He turns to me.

"Extraordinary room," he says.

"Yes," I say.

"You can't get this in London. This kind of — " he gestures — "mixing."

I look at where the gesture lands. The Chinese tables at the centre and back. The British tables near the door with the better light. Sofia moving between both with her Nansen passport in a room where James's British one means he answers to different laws than I do, on the same street, in the same city, on the same night.

"Yes," I say. "Extraordinary mixing."

The orchestra finishes a number. Then the opening bars of something slower — a melody I know from my grandmother's house, a folk song from Suzhou, now dressed in jazz harmonics and played on Western instruments for a room that has no idea where it came from. It is beautiful. It is also something taken apart and reassembled without asking permission. Both things are true at once, which is a phrase I use often in my mind and never say aloud to James.

He reaches across the table and takes my hand. He is genuinely happy tonight. He finds Shanghai exhilarating and I find it mine, which are feelings that look identical from the outside and are nothing alike.

Across the room Sofia delivers a bottle to a table of British officers, laughs at something, moves on. Katya will be asleep by now. Princess Vera will be waiting up, reading, in the coat she wears indoors because they cannot afford to heat the room properly. Tomorrow Sofia will be back here, in the midnight blue gown, at the next table, with the professional laugh.

The music plays on. The room is electric and briefly perfect.

Outside, the Bund glows along the river the foreign powers divided between themselves fifty years ago and have not yet returned.

James says it is the most modern city in Asia. He is not wrong. He is just not right in the way he thinks he is.
