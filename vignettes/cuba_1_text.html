In 1912 this room held forty people who all knew each other. The music was son — tres, bongos, maracas, a voice — and everyone understood the clave without being told. You could not explain clave to someone who didn't already have it in their body. You either grew up with it or you spent years catching the shadow of something you could hear but not find.

Now it holds a hundred and twenty. On a Friday, more.

I am Eduardo. I have owned this club near the Malecón since 1908. I have watched Havana change the way you watch a relative who is doing very well and becoming someone you don't entirely recognize.

The Americans began arriving before Prohibition — we were always interesting to them, always warm and close and available in ways their own country was not. But January 1920 was different. That month their government made alcohol illegal and the boats from Miami started arriving full. Forty-five thousand Americans came to Cuba in 1916. By 1928 it was ninety thousand. I know these numbers because they walked through my door.

What changed first was the clothing. In 1912 the men who came to this room wore their good guayaberas — the embroidered linen shirts that breathe in the heat, that a Cuban man wears when he wants to look correct without pretending the tropics are somewhere else. The women wore cotton, light colors, practical. Everyone was comfortable because no one was pretending to be anywhere but Havana in August.

The Americans arrived in wool. Actual wool, in this heat, because that is what you wear to a night out in New York and they had not yet understood that Havana is not New York with better rum. They sweated through their jackets and called it atmosphere. By the mid-twenties the smarter ones started copying us — linen suits, open collars — and I give them credit for that. Learning from a place instead of just consuming it is something.

The music changed more slowly, which is the nature of music. Son was already moving — out of the eastern provinces into Havana, into the clubs, being recorded for the first time. The Sexteto Habanero, Trío Matamoros. What had been neighborhood music was becoming something a city claimed. I don't regret that. The music got to more people and more people is generally correct for music.

What changed was the room's relationship to the clave.

When forty Cubans are dancing, the clave lives in the air between them. No one marks it visibly — it would be like marking your own heartbeat. The rhythm interlocks, the dancers move inside it, the musicians push against it and return. You feel the whole room breathing together on a count that isn't written anywhere.

When half the room is American, the clave becomes something the musicians have to carry alone. The Americans like the energy — they feel it, they respond to it, they order another daiquiri and lean toward the music with genuine pleasure. But they are outside the count. They hear the surface. The interior is still there, but now only half the room is living in it. The musicians know. You can hear them working slightly harder, holding the structure up for people who don't know it needs holding.

I don't say this against the Americans. They came here with thirst — for rum, yes, but also for something their own country was not giving them. A looseness. A permission. Their Prohibition was not only about alcohol. It was about control, about a certain kind of American deciding what another kind of American was allowed to enjoy. They came to Havana to exhale. I understand that. I have made a good living from it.

But I remember 1912. I remember forty people in this room who all had the clave in their bodies, and the music that rose from that — effortless, inevitable, a conversation in a language everyone present already spoke.

Now the orchestra is better. The sound system is better. The rum is better and there is more of it. My roof doesn't leak. I have six employees instead of two.

And some nights, late, when the Americans have gone back to the Sevilla-Biltmore and the room has contracted to the people who live here — the musicians relax into something different. The clave comes back up into the air where it belongs. The dancers find each other on the count. The music stops working and starts breathing.

For an hour it is 1912 again. Then the last ferry from Miami arrives and someone opens the door.
