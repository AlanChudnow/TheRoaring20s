I opened that show. People forget that now, or maybe they never knew it — they remember Josephine, which is correct, she's the one to remember — but the curtain went up on me. Just me, pushing a vegetable cart to center stage with the New York skyline painted behind me. Then I stopped, took out my clarinet, and played. That was how La Revue Nègre began on October 2, 1925, at the Théâtre des Champs-Élysées in Paris.

We came over on the Berengaria, the whole company — thirteen days on the Atlantic, rehearsing on deck when the weather allowed. She was nineteen. I was twenty-eight and had already been to Europe once, already knew what it felt like to play for people who listened differently than Americans did. She didn't know yet. She was about to find out.

The house was everything Paris puts in one room when it decides to pay attention. Evening dress from the front rows back to wherever evening dress ran out. The smell of perfume and anticipation. The kind of crowd that has heard there is something worth seeing and arrived ready to judge whether that is true. Janet Flanner was there from The New Yorker. Critics whose names I didn't know yet but would. Artists, writers, the whole French cultural machine assembled and running.

I played my solo. They listened the way the French listen — completely, with their whole bodies, as if music is a physical thing entering them, which it is, but most people don't know it. Then the scenes, the Charleston number, the company moving through the show. She danced the Charleston and they liked it. They didn't know yet what was coming.

The Danse Sauvage was the closing number. The show built to it. Jacques Charles had added it four days before we opened — pulled her out of the chorus line, told her she'd be performing nearly naked with Joe Alex as her partner, erotic and wild, closing the show. She told me later she was furious. She nearly quit. What they were asking her to do, the way they were framing it — savage, primitive, African — they were using her body to perform an idea Paris already had about Black people, and she knew it, and she was nineteen years old and three thousand miles from home and they were offering her two hundred and fifty dollars a week.

She did it. And she made it entirely her own.

I was in the pit when it happened. I was playing the music she was dancing through, which means I felt it before I saw it — the way the room changed above me, the particular silence that fell when Joe Alex carried her in upside down, doing the splits on his shoulder. He brought her center stage that way, his hands around her waist, and swung her slowly to the floor in a cartwheel, and she stood.

Feathers at her waist, her wrists, her ankles. Nothing else.

Then she started to move.

I have played for dancers my whole life. I know what it means when a dancer and the music find each other — really find each other, not just keeping time but thinking together, the body and the sound becoming one thing. That is what happened. She said afterward: I improvised, crazed by the music. Even my teeth and eyes burned with fever. That is what it felt like from inside the music too. We were burning together. The tempo she needed, I found. The intensity she was climbing toward, the horns climbed with her. You don't plan that. It either happens or it doesn't.

Above me I could hear Paris losing its mind one person at a time and then all at once. A scream went through that theatre — not fear, not shock exactly, something older than either of those, something that lives below thought. Then applause like I have not heard before or since, the kind that starts before a performance is finished because the audience cannot contain itself.

She thought the whistling on opening night meant they hated her. In Paris, whistling is disapproval — she didn't know that. She was standing backstage thinking she had failed while the audience was expressing the closest thing the French have to outrage-as-admiration. By the second week they were chanting her name.

I think about what it cost her, that dance. What Paris wanted from her — the savage, the primitive, the exotic — was not who she was. She was from St. Louis. She had grown up in poverty, survived things, danced in the streets for coins, clawed her way into shows that didn't want her, been told she was too dark and too thin and too strange. None of that was primitive. All of it was modern, American, specific, and hers.

But she took what they needed her to be and ran it through what she actually was, and what came out the other side was something neither one of those things alone could have made. That's what I was playing underneath. That's what the music was doing — holding the space where those two things met.

After, she was a star. Not gradually. That night. Paris decided between the Danse Sauvage beginning and ending that she was the one, and Paris doesn't unmake that decision once it makes it.

I knew her before any of that. I was there when the curtain came up, and I was in the pit when the curtain came down, and in between, something happened that I had a small part in making. That is enough for any musicaner. That is more than enough.
