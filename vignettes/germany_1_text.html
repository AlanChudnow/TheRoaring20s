The opera is dedicated to me. I should say that at the start, before I say anything else, because it colors everything. Alban Berg dedicated Wozzeck to me, and I paid for the piano score to be printed when no one else would, and so tonight at the Berlin Staatsoper I sit in my seat with the particular feeling of a person attending the birth of something they have already, in some sense, midwifed. It is not entirely comfortable.

I do not love atonal music. I will say that plainly. I loved Gustav's music — I love it still, with the helpless loyalty one has for the dead — and Gustav's music, for all its strangeness, was still music in the way I understand music: melody, development, resolution, the long arc of feeling from beginning to end. What Schoenberg has done, what his students have done, is something else. It dismantles the arc. It refuses the resolution. I find it cold more often than not.

And yet. And yet I paid. And yet I am here.

Because Berg is not Schoenberg. This is what I understood when I read the score — what I felt, I should say, because feeling was always my more reliable instrument. Berg takes the twelve-tone method and runs it through something warmer, something more theatrical, something closer to the human body and its particular suffering. The story is Büchner: a soldier, poverty, a woman, madness, murder, a child left alone at the end playing in the dirt while the other children run to see the body. No heroes. No transcendence. Just the thing itself.

Kleiber has given everything to this production. Thirty-four rehearsals — I heard it was thirty-four — when the house management wanted him to stop at half that number. He told them he would stake his position on it. Good. That is the only attitude worth having about work of this kind.

The house is full and divided. I can feel the divisions before the lights go down — the way certain people sit, the conversations that stop rather than continue when I pass. There are those who have come to be scandalized and those who have come to resist the scandal on behalf of tradition, and those — a smaller group, recognizable by a particular quality of stillness — who have come because they already know what they are about to hear and cannot quite believe it will happen in front of an audience of this size. I have been in rooms like this before. The premiere of Mahler's Sixth. Kokoschka's first show at the Kunstschau, where half Vienna wanted to throw him out and the other half wanted to marry him. History has a particular humidity before it breaks.

When the orchestra begins it does not begin in the way one expects. There is no invitation. The sound is already inside something — already inside a problem — and you are placed inside it whether you wish to be or not. Fragments assemble and dissolve. Phrases arrive and do not complete themselves in the expected way. I sit very still. This is, I understand within the first minutes, not incompetence. It is a different grammar of feeling. Berg is not confused. He is describing a world that has lost its grammar and showing you what that sounds like from the inside.

The story does not offer comfort because Büchner did not offer comfort because the world he described does not contain it. Wozzeck is humiliated by his officers, experimented on by his doctor, betrayed by his wife, driven past reason into murder and then into the water. The child at the end does not understand what has happened. The other children run to see. That is where it stops.

Around me the room is doing what rooms do at difficult premieres. A program lowered, then raised. A small sound — not quite a cough, not quite disapproval — from somewhere to my left. Someone leaving, carefully, during the second act. Someone else, near the front, sitting forward in a way that means they have stopped thinking about themselves and started thinking only about what is happening on the stage. That is the thing one watches for. That is the sign.

I have been told I do not understand modern music. Gustav told me this, in the early years, though he said it more gently than others have managed. Schoenberg has implied it. Various critics, writing about my life rather than engaging with my mind, have assumed it. What they mean is that I do not accept modern music on faith, which is different. I require that it earn what it asks of me. Tonight something is being asked, and I am sitting very still, and I am not certain I can refuse it.

One trembled. That is the truest thing I can say about it. One trembled, as before something unheard of.

When it ends the applause is real but uneven — some immediate, some delayed, some watching others before deciding. This is correct. Unanimous applause at a premiere like this would mean the audience had not understood. The arguments begin in the aisles before the house lights are fully up. Also correct.

Berg finds me afterwards, his face the particular face of a man who has been waiting for a verdict for four years and is still not sure he wants to hear it. I take his hands.

I tell him the truth: that I do not love it the way I love Gustav, that I am not certain I ever will, and that none of that matters, because what he has made cannot be unmade, and the world of music is now a different shape than it was three hours ago, and I paid for it, and it is dedicated to me, and I am proud.

He looks as if he might weep. Franz takes my arm and we go out into the cold Berlin night, and I think: I have been present at the beginning of things before. One learns to recognize the feeling. It sits in the chest like a stone and like a light at the same time.
