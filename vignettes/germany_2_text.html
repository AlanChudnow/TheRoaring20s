I am in Berlin because I told my father I wanted to study the language, and he believed me, which speaks well of him and perhaps not so well of me. I have been here four months. My German is adequate for ordering things and poor for understanding things. This turns out to be enough.

I. The Threepenny Opera — Theater am Schiffbauerdamm

Tonight began at the Theater am Schiffbauerdamm. A man I know slightly — Friedrich, a painter, the kind who talks about painting more than he paints — had a spare ticket for The Threepenny Opera and pressed it on me over coffee. I accepted because I had nothing else to do, which is how most things happen here.

The Threepenny Opera is not like other evenings in the theatre. My first thought, settling into the seat, was that it had something of Gilbert and Sullivan about it — the same bones, you might say, or Offenbach: songs inserted into dialogue, comic characters carrying a satirical point, the whole dressed up as entertainment so the argument can enter without announcing itself. But where Gilbert and Sullivan mock gently and Offenbach is simply merry, Kurt Weill's music turns on you. The songs sound like songs you already know until they suddenly don't. A melody arrives cheerful and departs cold.

The opening ballad is the clearest example. It concerns a gentleman named Macheath — Mack the Knife, they call him — and it catalogues his crimes with the brisk good cheer one might use to describe a walking tour of the city. Shark teeth. A jackknife. A body on the embankment. The melody meanwhile is doing everything it can to make you tap your foot, and the terrible thing is that it succeeds. I caught myself nodding along to a verse about a murder and stopped, and looked around, and found that half the audience had not stopped.

The orchestra is small and the sound is slightly wrong — deliberately, I think, though I wasn't certain at first. A man next to me laughed at a line I found upsetting. The woman behind me sat completely still at a moment when half the theatre was laughing. I spent as much time watching the audience as the stage. This is, I have come to understand, how Berlin wants you to watch things.

Later, walking home, I understood it better. London has Jack the Ripper — had him, rather, forty years ago, a monster safely historical, his crimes turned into penny dreadfuls and guided tours of Whitechapel. Berlin has something different. The Germans have a word: Lustmord. Erotic murder. Not a metaphor — a category, with its own cultural life. Fritz Haarmann, the Vampire of Hanover, was convicted four years ago — children sang rhymes about him in the street while his trial was still running. Peter Kürten is operating in Düsseldorf right now, as I sit in this theatre, though he has not yet been caught. George Grosz paints mutilated women. Otto Dix paints them. The newspapers describe the killers in such loving detail that the line between horror and fascination dissolved some time ago and nobody has thought to redraw it.

So when Brecht puts a charming murderer in an operetta and sets him to an irresistible tune, he is not importing something foreign into German culture. He is reflecting something already there — the city's own image, held up at a particular angle. The woman beside me pressed her handkerchief to her mouth, from laughing I think, though I could not be entirely certain. That ambiguity seemed, on reflection, to be precisely the point. Brecht wants you to enjoy it. He also wants you to notice that you are enjoying it. Both at once, with nowhere comfortable to stand.

Walking home I passed two men in brown shirts on a corner, standing with the particular stillness of men who are waiting for something. I have been seeing more of them lately. The newspapers treat political violence and criminal violence with the same appetite — the same column inches, the same breathless detail — and I found myself wondering, not for the first time, whether a city that has learned to find murder aesthetically interesting might be a city that finds it easier to accept as politically useful. I don't know the answer. I'm not sure anyone here is asking the question.

II. The Club — Somewhere Below Street Level

Afterwards the street felt very cold and very bright. Berlin has a particular quality at night — the electric lights don't soften anything, they simply show it to you. I stood outside for a moment trying to locate Friedrich, who had vanished, and found myself instead next to a young man in a good coat who said something to me in German I didn't catch. I said I was English. He said his English was better than my German. He was right. His name was Karl and he knew a place.

The place was a club with a low ceiling and no name on the door that I could see. You went down half a flight of stairs. The smoke was serious — the kind that has been there long enough to become part of the room. Karl installed me at a small table near the back and bought two drinks without asking what I wanted. The drinks were clear and tasted of aniseed and I drank mine carefully.

The master of ceremonies came out first. A small man in a tailcoat and heavy powder, with eyes that had seen everything and found it, on balance, amusing. He told a joke in German. The Germans laughed. He told it again in French — slightly differently, I noticed, the punchline adjusted, a different kind of joke now. The French table in the corner laughed. Then he turned to the room at large and said something in English about all of us being very welcome, said it with a particular emphasis on the word welcome that suggested he knew exactly what kind of welcome he was extending and to whom. I felt simultaneously included and lightly mocked, which I would later understand is precisely what he was trying to achieve.

Then the performers. Boys in corsets and heavy eye paint, monocles worn with complete commitment. Girls in dinner jackets with their hair cropped to the ear, moving with a loose authority that made their male partners look tentative. The costumes suggested a great deal and confirmed nothing — feathers at some throats, military epaulettes worn over bare shoulders, stockings visible where stockings were not normally visible. Not scandalous, quite. More like: the room has its own rules and has had them for some time and you have just arrived.

The songs were about money. About what men do for it and what it no longer buys. One performer — a woman, I believe, though I could not have sworn to it — sang something that Karl translated for me in fragments: she was playing a woman who sold herself but refused, in the song, to use the word for what she sold, because the word for money had become more shameful than the act. The room understood this. I was still working it out when the applause began.

A comedian did a sketch about a government official so thoroughly that two men near the front stopped laughing at the same moment, as if remembering something. Other songs followed — sharper, shorter, more direct than anything in the theatre. Brecht, Karl told me, was a different project from Weill. The theatre was argument. This was evidence.

I am a reasonable person. I have been to the theatre, to concerts, to a number of things that could be described as culture. I do not think I have seen anything like this. Not because it was shocking — it wasn't, quite — but because it wasn't trying to be. It was simply saying something, in a room full of people who had also come to hear it said.

Karl watched me watching. At some point he said: "You look like someone who has just learned a word they didn't know they were missing."

I didn't answer. He was right, and I wasn't sure I wanted to say so.

III. After Hours — Somewhere Else Entirely

Later — much later, in the way that time works differently here after midnight — we were somewhere else. An apartment or a back room, I wasn't entirely certain. Friedrich had reappeared. There were six or seven people and then there were more. Someone played the piano for a while, not performing, just playing. Then the piano stopped and someone put a record on the phonograph — the Comedian Harmonists, six male voices doing something technically improbable with a popular song, the harmonies stacking and dissolving so cleanly it sounded like one instrument being played by several people who shared a mind. Another record followed: Josephine Baker, unmistakable even at low volume, a record someone had brought back from Paris. The room went quiet in a particular way when Baker came on — not reverent, exactly, but attentive, the way you attend to something you know is significant without quite knowing why.

Conversations started and subdivided. A woman argued about Brecht with great force and then laughed suddenly and the argument was over.

I sat for a long time without saying much, which no one seemed to mind. This is one of the things I have come to appreciate about Berlin — it does not require you to perform contentment. You can simply be present, which is a different thing.

At some point Karl sat down beside me and we talked for an hour about, I think, everything except what we were actually talking about. Outside, the city went on being itself. By the time I left it was beginning to get light.

I walked back through streets that were not quite empty. A tram passed. A woman sold newspapers from a corner. Somewhere someone was still playing music, faint enough that I couldn't identify it.

I thought: I should write this down. I thought: how do you write down a feeling that you don't have a word for yet.

I am working on it.
