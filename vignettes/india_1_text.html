I. Dorothy Whitmore — Wife of the Deputy Commissioner

My husband said we were attending a musical evening at Mr. Mehta's house and that it would be polite to come. He said this the way he says most things about India — as a duty to be discharged pleasantly. I put on the ivory silk and we went.

The house in Malabar Hill was larger than I expected and arranged for the occasion — cushions and low seats around a cleared space, oil lamps rather than electric light, the smell of jasmine from somewhere I couldn't locate. Perhaps forty people. Indians in fine dress — the women in silk saris that caught the lamplight, the men in achkan coats. Three or four British couples, us among them, seated slightly apart in the way that happens without anyone arranging it.

Then she came in.

I knew who she was — one hears the name, even in our circles. Gauhar Jan. The gramophone woman, Reginald calls her, though not unkindly. She was older than I'd imagined, and more — I want to say formidable, though that's not quite it. She moved through the room as if she had assessed it in one glance and found it adequate. She wore deep red silk, heavy gold at her ears and throat, her hair oiled and dressed with flowers. She sat, adjusted her position once, and nodded to the accompanists.

What followed I cannot properly describe, which I find embarrassing to admit. I am not unmusical. I play piano. I know what I like. But this was something I had no framework for — the way her voice moved through intervals I couldn't anticipate, curling around a note and then releasing it somewhere unexpected, the tabla underneath marking a rhythm that seemed to breathe rather than beat. The other guests were still in a particular way — not politely still, genuinely still, the way one is still when the mind has stopped performing attention and simply arrived at it.

At some point I realized I had no idea how long she had been singing. Time had done something unusual. I didn't mention this to Reginald on the way home. He said it had been very pleasant. I agreed.

But I kept hearing it. Three days later, I was still hearing it.

II. Uday Shankar — Home from London and Paris, Still Looking

I came back to India in 1927 not knowing exactly what I was looking for, which is the honest version. The less honest version is that I came back to find the real India, which is what I told people in Paris, and which is the kind of thing you say in Paris without embarrassing yourself.

London gave me ballet. It gave me Pavlova — a Russian woman who understood something about Indian movement that I, who grew up with it, had stopped seeing because I'd always had it. She made me look at Indian sculpture at the British Museum and ask: what does this hand position mean as a movement? What does this figure weight mean in a body? Questions you don't ask about the water you swim in.

The mehfil at Mehta's house was the kind of evening I grew up attending and then spent five years in Europe romanticizing. Sitting in the lamplight I felt both things at once — the genuine recognition of being home, and the slight foreignness of someone who has been away long enough to see the familiar clearly.

Gauhar Jan began and the room organized itself around her voice the way a room always does when something real is happening in it. I watched with the particular attention I've developed — half experiencing, half analyzing, which Pavlova taught me and which is both useful and slightly melancholy as a way of being in the world.

What she was doing with the thumri was architectural. The way she constructed a phrase and then dismantled it, the way she held a note past where you expected resolution and then resolved it sideways into something you hadn't anticipated — this was not decoration. This was argument. A sophisticated, sequential argument made entirely of sound, requiring no translation for anyone in that room except possibly the British couple near the window, who looked moved in the slightly puzzled way of people experiencing something they recognize as significant without knowing precisely why.

I thought about Covent Garden. About the Royal Opera House audience leaning forward when Pavlova and I performed Radha and Krishna — their attention genuine but reaching across a distance, the appreciative distance of people encountering something from elsewhere. Here there was no distance. The music and the room were the same thing.

I have been trying to build a bridge between those two experiences ever since I left London. Sitting in Mehta's drawing room listening to Gauhar Jan, I understood that the bridge is not the point. The point is to know both shores.

III. Gauhar Jan — Afterward

I have performed for the Nizam of Hyderabad and the King of England. I have sung into a brass horn in a hotel room in Calcutta while a young Englishman named Fred Gaisberg cranked a machine that would put my voice on a disc and sell it to people I would never meet. I charge three thousand rupees for an evening. I have earned more money than most men in this country will see in a lifetime, and I have spent most of it, and the Gramophone Company has kept the rest, which is the arrangement we made and which I understood better afterward than I did at the time.

Mehta's mehfil was a pleasant room. Good acoustics, which one notices immediately. The tabla player knows his business. The tanpura was slightly flat but we corrected it before I began and no one else would have heard the difference.

I sang for two hours. Bhairavi at the end, which is always the end, which is the raga of farewell. By then the room was very quiet — the good quiet, not the polite quiet, and one always knows the difference.

Afterward three people found me before I could leave.

The young man first — the dancer, Shankar's boy, the one who has been in London. He spoke about what I had done with the bandish in a way that told me he had actually heard it, which is rarer than one would wish. He asked about the ornamentation in the third section. We spoke for perhaps ten minutes. He has been looking at Indian dance from the outside long enough that he can describe its interior precisely. This is useful. This is also a kind of sadness.

Then the British woman, the commissioner's wife. She had that slightly overwhelmed expression I have seen many times — not condescension, something more honest than that. She said she didn't have the words. I told her the music doesn't require them. She looked as if this helped, though I'm not certain it did.

Then a young man I didn't recognize pressed a gramophone record into my hands — one of mine, old, from 1906, the paper sleeve worn at the corner. He said his father had played it until the needle had gone through the groove. He said nothing else. He didn't need to.

My name is Gauhar Jaan.

I have always announced it at the end. The Gramophone Company asked me to, those first sessions in the hotel room, so that listeners would know whose voice they were hearing. I said it then and I have said it since — at the end of every performance, every recording, every room I have filled with this voice and then emptied again.

My name is Gauhar Jaan. Remember it.
