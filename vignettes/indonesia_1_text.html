Campuhan, Ubud, Bali — October 1928
Dear Mother,

I have been meaning to write properly for months and something always interrupts — a ceremony, a painter who needs help with a commission, the Dutch officials who arrive with notebooks and leave with the air of men who have verified something without understanding it. Tonight the house is quiet. The two rivers run below my window. I will try to describe what happened three nights ago, though I should warn you that I will probably fail.

You know that I came to Java five years ago knowing very little, and learned gamelan at the Yogyakarta palace, and began to understand — only began — what it means that music can be organized differently from everything I was taught in Moscow or Dresden. In Germany we think of music as a line. A melody goes from here to there. Harmony supports it. Rhythm carries it. Everything serves the forward motion. The gamelan is not a line. It is a field. Multiple patterns occupy the same space simultaneously, each one interlocking with the others so precisely that if one player stops, the whole structure collapses. Not supports — actually collapses, becomes incoherent. I spent two years in Yogyakarta before I understood this not just intellectually but physically, in my body, and even then I understood it only in the Javanese form.

Balinese gamelan is faster. Brighter. More metallic — the bronze keys ring rather than sing. When I first heard it, arriving here fourteen months ago, I thought I understood it because I had learned the Javanese version. I was wrong in exactly the way a man is wrong when he thinks knowing one language means knowing another because they share an alphabet.

Three nights ago there was a performance in the temple courtyard at the edge of the village. Not for tourists — there were no other Europeans present. The Tjokorda had told me it was happening and I simply went and sat where there was room. The torches threw orange light onto the stone. The gamelan began and the sound went immediately into the chest — not metaphorically, physically. The bronze at that volume and that density does something to the air that you feel before you hear it.

Then the dancers came.

I have been trying for fourteen months to draw what Balinese dancers do with their eyes and their fingers and I cannot do it. The eyes move independently of the head. The fingers bend backward at angles that suggest a different relationship between the human hand and its possibilities. The body is not relaxed into the music the way a Western dancer might relax — it is charged, every segment separately controlled, the torso doing one thing while the arms do another and the eyes do a third. And yet the whole is not fragmented. It is more unified than anything I have seen in a European theatre precisely because every part is fully present.

There was a moment — perhaps an hour into the performance, I had lost track — when a young dancer went into what I can only describe as a trance. She was still dancing but something had changed in how she was present. The musicians around her shifted, not dramatically, a change in intensity rather than pattern. The sound became what I wrote in my notebook afterward: infinitely sinuous winding melody. I don't know if that means anything to you. It meant something I cannot fully translate.

I thought about Berlin. About the concerts I attended as a student, the way a European audience sits in the dark facing a stage, the music flowing outward from the performers and the audience receiving it. A transaction. Here there was no separation of that kind. The courtyard, the musicians, the dancers, the torchlight, the temple walls, the people sitting and the people standing — it was one thing happening in one place and I was inside it rather than in front of it.

Now I must tell you something I have been thinking about carefully, because I do not want to be dishonest with you or with myself.

I am a European on a Dutch colonial island. I did not choose the Dutch colonial system and I do not admire it — the officials who come with their notebooks treat the Balinese the way one treats an interesting problem rather than a civilization that has been managing itself with considerable sophistication for longer than Holland has existed. But I benefit from the system whether I choose it or not. The roads I travel, the relative freedom I enjoy, the tolerance extended to my particular nature — all of this exists within a structure that is not neutral. When I sit in that temple courtyard and feel that I have understood something, I must be careful about what I think I have understood and what I am simply consuming.

What I can say honestly is this: I am not a tourist here. I am learning the language. I am learning the music — I have begun notating the gamelan the way I notated the Javanese at the palace, and the musicians seem to find this amusing and not offensive. I am painting. I am trying to be useful without being extractive, which is harder than it sounds and probably never fully achievable.

The Tjokorda has been generous and patient with my slow understanding. There is a young dancer named Limbak who is extraordinary — I watch him the way I imagine a musician watches a prodigy, with the particular mixture of admiration and curiosity about what is possible. We are beginning to work on something together, though I cannot yet say what it is.

What I know is that I will not leave this island. That was decided three nights ago in the temple courtyard, if it had not been decided already. Something happened in that music that I do not have the language to describe, in German or in any other language I speak. So I will stay until I find it.

The rivers are very loud tonight. The rains have been heavy. Please give my love to everyone.

Your Walter
