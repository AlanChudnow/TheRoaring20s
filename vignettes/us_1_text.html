Ask anyone on 133rd Street — Willie can move. That was a fact before the night I'm telling you about, and it was still a fact after. But somewhere in between, Josephine Baker got to the floor, and for about forty minutes I might as well have been standing in a doorway watching.

The party was a third-floor walkup, rent money at the door. Room already too full when I got there — coats on the bed nobody would sleep in, grease smell from whatever was cooking in back, heat rising off forty bodies before the music even started. Piano jammed against the wall. Trumpet so close it could take your ear off. Floor bouncing under all that weight, creaking like it was making up its mind.

I took the floor when the space cleared. Gave them the shorty george, clean knee knocks, a little mess around at the end. The crowd was with me. I could feel it the way you always can — the room tightening, people leaning in. I was getting hot and I knew it.

Then Josephine started moving.

She went low first — that was the thing that caught you. Where other dancers stood proud, she bent her knees and sank, like the ground was pulling her in and she was glad of it. Elbows out, limbs all at angles, akimbo from head to foot. Then the snake hips. Lord. Not side to side like most — everything moving separate, like each part of her body had its own particular mind and none of them were talking to the others. She hit the shimmy and her shoulders went loose as wash on a line while her feet kept a whole different conversation going underneath. The woman had rubber where other people had bone.

I answered her. Matched her step for step and put something of my own on top of it. She took that and went somewhere I hadn't been. I went after her. She was already gone again.

This went on. The trumpet player was grinning. The piano man had stopped sweating and started playing to her specifically — I could hear it — following where she led, speeding when she sped. The crowd forgot to watch me, then forgot I was there.

Then she did the thing with her eyes.

Body still going — hips, shoulders, feet, all of it — but her eyes went somewhere separate. Not at the crowd, not at me, not at anything in that room. Laughing at something private. Mocking something only she could see. The whole room felt it at once, like a congregation when the spirit drops. You can't teach that. You can't copy it. You either have it behind the steps or you don't.

I stopped trying to beat her and just watched.

She danced until the floor was slick and the candles were guttering from all those moving bodies and the piano man's shirt was soaked clean through. She looked like she could have kept going till morning. Like the heat was just more fuel.

No winner called. That's not how it works. But there wasn't a soul in that room who didn't know.

Two years later she was in Paris. Théâtre des Champs-Élysées. All of Europe losing its mind, her picture in every paper. I read about it and thought: I already knew. She showed me on 133rd Street, and I was paying close attention.
