You want to know what this business is? I'll tell you what this business is. It's pianos. Forty pianos in forty rooms, all playing different songs at the same time, and somewhere in that noise is next year's money. You learn to hear it. After thirty years, you learn.

Berlin is in the third room on the left. I don't have to check — I can hear him through the wall. That touch. Precise, like a man who knows exactly how much a note is worth and won't spend a penny more. He's working on something new, this Puttin' On the Ritz business. Already I can hear it's got legs. Clean melody, strong rhythm, nothing that confuses a person. You can whistle it leaving the building. That's the whole game, you understand? Can they whistle it? Berlin, they can always whistle it.

He came to me with nothing, that boy. An immigrant kid from the Lower East Side, couldn't read music properly, played only in F-sharp because that's where the black keys are. Nu, so what did he do? He wrote Alexander's Ragtime Band and I sold a million copies. A million. From F-sharp. Don't talk to me about limitations.

Now Gershwin — Gershwin is a different story. Also brilliant, I'm not saying not brilliant. But brilliant in a way that costs me sleep. He's in the next room and already I can hear the mishegoss he's up to. Longer phrases, funny chords that don't land where they should, harmonies going sideways when they should go straight. He's thinking about Carnegie Hall when I need him thinking about dance halls. There's a difference. A significant difference.

I told him last week: George, a song is a door. You open it, you walk through, it closes behind you — clean, finished, done. What you're writing is a whole house. Beautiful, maybe. But people don't buy houses on a Wednesday afternoon, they buy doors.

He smiled at me the way young men smile at old men when they think the old man doesn't understand. Maybe he's right. What do I know? I only sold ten million pieces of sheet music.

This building right now — listen to it. Every room is somebody trying to write the thing that sticks. A sentimental ballad coming from upstairs, probably gornisht, too slow. A comedy number from down the hall, could be something, the rhythm is right. Two men arguing about a chorus on the second floor — arguing is good, arguing means they care. The ones who don't argue, their songs are also nothing.

What people don't understand about this street, about what we do here, is that the glamour is somewhere else. The glamour is in the club, on the stage, in the picture house. Here is where the sausage gets made. Paper, ink, piano keys, sweat. By five o'clock my ears are full of other people's melodies and I can't remember which ones I heard today and which ones I heard in 1911.

But then Berlin plays something through that wall and it cuts right through. That's the thing about a real song — it cuts. Everything else is just noise around it.

Nu. Back to work. The gelt doesn't count itself.
